Use case: digital care programs and routine monitoring

In community programs and clinic-linked digital care, teams need a functional baseline that is easy to repeat. AndanteFit operationalizes the SPPB in a consistent format so results can be used for triage, individualized exercise planning, and longitudinal follow-up without adding heavy staff burden.

Who uses it

Non-specialist staff

Designed for frontline teams who need simple, repeatable workflows with minimal training.

Where it runs

Community + clinic touchpoints

Suitable for periodic check-ins at centers, outpatient clinics, and research follow-up visits.

What it supports

Tracking change over time

Repeated measures help detect functional decline early and document response to intervention.

Typical workflow

1) Intake and baseline

  • Run a standardized SPPB session (gait speed, five-times sit-to-stand, balance).
  • Use the score and component results to stratify program intensity.
  • Capture notes for contraindications or mobility aids.

2) Follow-up and reporting

  • Repeat the same protocol at fixed intervals (e.g., every 4–12 weeks).
  • Compare component-level changes to guide exercise progression.
  • Generate simple summaries for clinicians, program managers, or study teams.

Outputs teams usually need

Mobility

Gait speed and timing

Reliable timing and consistent start/stop logic support comparable measurements across operators.

Muscle power

Sit-to-stand performance

Component results help distinguish slow mobility from limited lower-limb power.

Balance

Stability and stance

Balance subtests provide practical signals for fall-risk education and targeted training.

Validation context

AndanteFit is an example of an automated system designed to operationalize the SPPB in clinical and research settings. Its validation involved Dr. Jack Guralnik, who originally developed the SPPB during his work at the U.S. National Institutes of Health. In addition, Professor Alfonso Cruz-Jentoft and colleagues from the Global Leadership Initiative on Sarcopenia (GLIS) participated in the validation process, supporting its applicability in studies involving older adults.
