Sarcopenia Management in Transition: The Korean Experience

International consensus is shifting from a muscle mass–centered definition toward a function-centered approach. Within this evolving landscape, Korea's sarcopenia guidelines offer a pragmatic interpretation — one that prioritizes objective functional assessment and a continuous care model.

Sarcopenia can no longer be explained simply by the loss of muscle mass. Recent international discussions have redefined it as a clinical syndrome encompassing reduced muscle strength and physical performance, and this shift affects not only diagnostic criteria but also screening strategies, treatment targets, and care delivery design. Korea's sarcopenia guidelines have drawn on these international developments while proposing several practical adaptations suited to the realities of clinical and community care in Korea. This article summarizes those changes, Korea's approach, and the challenges that remain.

For a long time, discussions around sarcopenia focused heavily on the question of diagnostic thresholds. More recently, however, the framing has shifted. What matters now is not only how to define sarcopenia, but how to detect functional decline and poor health outcomes earlier — and how to connect that detection to sustained management. In this sense, sarcopenia needs to be understood not merely as a disorder of body composition, but as a functional problem linked to impaired mobility, falls, disability, hospitalization, and death.

Shifting Diagnostic Frameworks

Changes in international guidelines reflect this conceptual shift. Early criteria considered both muscle mass and functional decline, but placed relatively greater weight on mass. EWGSOP2 (Europe) subsequently moved muscle weakness to the forefront, using low muscle mass to confirm the diagnosis and physical performance to assess severity. AWGS 2019 (Asia) more actively incorporated physical performance into the diagnostic framework, elevating tools such as the SPPB to a meaningful role in the diagnostic process, in recognition of the characteristics of Asian populations. By contrast, AWGS 2025 reorganized its diagnostic criteria back around muscle strength and mass, reclassifying physical performance as an outcome measure. The broader international trajectory has been moving toward a clearer separation between diagnostic criteria and outcome assessment.

Against this backdrop, Korea's sarcopenia guidelines stand out for offering not merely an adoption of international standards, but a pragmatic interpretation tailored to the realities of Korean clinical and community settings. Those realities are uneven: assessment experience and access to equipment vary across institutions, and the degree of integration between acute care hospitals, primary care, and community services is inconsistent. In that environment, even the most carefully constructed diagnostic criteria may fail to translate into actual screening or management.

One notable feature of the Korean guidelines is the forward positioning of objective functional assessment. Making tools such as grip strength, gait speed, and the Timed Up and Go (TUG) test more broadly available at the screening stage is not simply an expansion of the assessment checklist. It reflects an effort to capture functional decline earlier and to build a clinically workable assessment structure. While this approach may carry some limitations in terms of strict international comparability, it reads as an appropriate choice within the Korean context, given its emphasis on early detection and feasibility.

The most distinctive conceptual contribution is the notion of functional sarcopenia — a clinical category defined by reduced muscle strength and physical performance in the presence of preserved muscle mass. The core insight is that patients who show early functional decline but have not yet crossed the threshold of low muscle mass may already belong to a clinically at-risk group. In practice, functional indicators tend to correlate more closely than muscle mass with outcomes such as mobility limitation, mortality, and institutionalization-free survival. The concept thus represents an attempt to bring patients with clear functional decline under clinical attention earlier, rather than leaving them outside the muscle mass–centered classification system.

This is less about Korea taking a divergent path from international standards and more about addressing a dimension — functional decline — that international consensus has treated as secondary. For frontline clinicians encountering patients in community and primary care settings, knowing how to interpret and act on functional deterioration that becomes apparent before muscle mass measurements are even taken is critically important. In that sense, functional sarcopenia may lack full international standardization, but it carries real explanatory value in clinical and public health practice.

That said, this is also an area that will require revision and refinement. Recent international guidelines are trending toward treating physical performance as an outcome measure rather than a diagnostic criterion. The function-centered screening and classification framework that Korean guidelines have emphasized may remain clinically useful, but maintaining international compatibility going forward will require revisiting the concepts, terminology, and scope of application. This is less a weakness of the Korean approach than an illustration of the ongoing challenge of balancing local applicability with global coherence.

Treatment and Management: Exercise and Nutrition

The direction of treatment and management is reasonably consistent across guidelines. The core interventions are exercise and nutrition. Pharmacological treatment remains a marginal component of routine clinical care. Of all available interventions, progressive resistance exercise has the strongest evidence base across the core domains of muscle mass, muscle strength, and physical performance. The generally recommended approach involves two to three sessions per week, eight to twelve repetitions, one to three sets, at moderate to high intensity. In practice, however, adjustments are needed based on the patient's baseline function, comorbidities, pain, adherence, and preferences. What matters is not rigid adherence to a single protocol, but the design of individualized interventions grounded in sound principles.

Exercise is not limited to resistance training. Aerobic exercise benefits cardiovascular fitness, overall performance capacity, and quality of life, but its effects on muscle strength and mass are limited, making it insufficient as a standalone intervention. Balance training, when combined with resistance exercise, extends functional gains and contributes to fall prevention. Flexibility training plays a supporting role in injury prevention and exercise performance rather than offering direct therapeutic effects on sarcopenia. In clinical and community program settings, the most realistic approach is to anchor the intervention in resistance training and layer in aerobic, balance, and flexibility components according to individual patient needs.

Nutritional management deserves equal emphasis alongside exercise. Inadequate protein intake is associated with increased sarcopenia risk, and sufficient protein supply is the most fundamental nutritional intervention. For generally healthy older adults, daily intake of 1.0–1.2 g/kg is recommended; for those with acute or chronic illness, 1.2–1.5 g/kg; and in cases of severe illness, up to 2.0 g/kg/day. Korean expert consensus similarly recommends a minimum of 1.2 g/kg/day for prevention purposes. Specific supplements may serve a complementary role in certain contexts, but the foundation of nutritional management remains the practical achievement of adequate total protein intake within the structure of everyday meals and lifestyle. Exercise and nutrition are not separate interventions — they should be designed together as components of a single management strategy.

The role of pharmacological treatment is limited. Testosterone therapy may be discussed in a restricted way for men with confirmed hypogonadism, but it cannot be considered routine treatment for sarcopenia broadly. Selective androgen receptor modulators, myostatin inhibitors, and ghrelin agonists are subjects of ongoing investigation, but at present none has accumulated the evidence needed to reshape standard practice. This reinforces the point that the center of sarcopenia management remains exercise, nutrition, and the systems that deliver them.

Care Delivery and Korea's Remaining Challenges

The most pressing issue, in practice, is the delivery system. In many cases, the barrier is not insufficient evidence but a structural gap between evidence and practice. Community and primary care settings need to serve as the starting points for early screening and initial intervention, while acute care hospitals should function as hubs for inpatient screening and diagnosis, comprehensive geriatric assessment, and post-discharge linkage. For screening, relatively accessible tools such as SARC-F, calf circumference, chair stand test, grip strength, gait speed, TUG, and SPPB can be deployed. Confirmation requires muscle mass measurement by BIA or DXA. This should then connect to comprehensive geriatric assessment — capturing comorbidities, functional status, depression, medications, and environmental risk factors — and feed into multicomponent exercise and nutrition interventions.

At this point, Korea's remaining challenges come into sharper focus. The problem is not a lack of diagnostic criteria — it is that the pathway after diagnosis is insufficiently connected. In primary care settings, awareness of diagnostic criteria and willingness to diagnose remain low. On the patient side, awareness of sarcopenia and access to information about available community services are limited. Added to this is the structural fragmentation of acute care, primary care, and community services, and the variation across regions in the content and sustainability of programs. When a patient is identified but the pathway forward is broken, even a sophisticated diagnostic system will fail to produce meaningful clinical outcomes.

On the other hand, Korea's experience also shows what becomes possible when a delivery system is in place. Community-based interventions in domestic studies have demonstrated improvements in muscle strength and physical performance, delayed progression of disability, and encouraging signals regarding cost-effectiveness. Population-level group exercise programs, protein supplementation, nutritional counseling, and multicomponent interventions illustrate that management need not end at the hospital door — it can evolve into a sustainable model embedded in daily life. The task ahead is not to add new criteria, but to standardize early screening, diagnosis, comprehensive assessment, and community linkage into a single continuous care model.

What is needed now is not greater complexity in diagnostic frameworks. It is a structure that understands sarcopenia through the lens of function, deploys objective functional assessment earlier in the care pathway, and connects exercise and nutrition interventions sustainably within both health systems and communities. Korea's approach demonstrates an effort to go beyond adoption of international standards and translate them into an executable local strategy. At the same time, it makes visible the continuing challenge of maintaining coherence with evolving international standards. In that sense, Korea's experience is best read not as a finished model, but as a case study in the ongoing process of translating global consensus into real-world practice.

The next step in sarcopenia management is not the creation of additional diagnostic criteria, but the sustainable implementation of function-based assessment and exercise and nutrition interventions within actual health and community systems.
